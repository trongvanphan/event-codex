# Personas

## Persona 1: Minh, the Convenience-First Organizer

### Descriptive

Minh is a 31-year-old product manager in Ho Chi Minh City. He organizes badminton for four to six colleagues after work, but coordination often consumes more time than the booking itself. He is tech-savvy, uses mobile wallets, and will pay a little more for certainty and convenience.

### Motivations

- Maintain a healthy weekly routine
- Spend less time coordinating people and venues
- Secure reliable courts near work or home
- Avoid collecting cash from every player

### Interactions

- Mobile-first; uses Zalo for group coordination
- Usually books weekday evenings two to five days ahead
- Repeats venues that are reliable
- Prefers one-tap rebooking and calendar reminders

### Pain Points

- Peak slots disappear quickly
- Availability is fragmented across venue messages
- Friends change plans after he has paid
- Cheapest is not always best if travel or quality is poor

### Moments that Matter

- Seeing a suitable court with a clear final price
- Confirming the court before the group loses interest
- Splitting payment and replacing a player
- Receiving a useful alternative when the preferred time is full

## Persona 2: Lan, the Price-Flexible Student

### Descriptive

Lan is a 21-year-old university student in Da Nang with an irregular class schedule and a limited recreation budget. She plays pickleball or badminton socially and can sometimes move a session to a quieter hour, but not every morning or afternoon is free.

### Motivations

- Play more often without exceeding her budget
- Meet compatible players
- Discover safe, accessible venues
- Receive genuine student value without complicated coupons

### Interactions

- Discovers activities through TikTok, Facebook, and friends
- Compares price visually and shares options in group chat
- Uses mobile payment but is sensitive to deposits and refund delays
- Responds to short, timely offers

### Pain Points

- Cannot always gather enough players
- Low-price slots may be too far away
- Student status does not accurately predict her daily schedule
- Hidden fees damage trust

### Moments that Matter

- Understanding total per-person cost
- Seeing "save 40,000 VND by playing at 15:00"
- Joining an open game with similar skill
- Receiving a fast refund or replacement after a change

## Persona 3: Huy, the Flexible Solo Player

### Descriptive

Huy is a 27-year-old freelance designer in Hanoi. His availability changes weekly, and he often wants to play during off-peak hours. His main obstacle is not price or inventory, but finding people at a compatible level.

### Motivations

- Turn flexible time into regular exercise
- Find balanced, friendly games
- Build a trusted player network
- Avoid inactive chat groups

### Interactions

- Searches on mobile at short notice
- Will travel farther for a good game
- Values ratings, skill labels, and attendance reliability
- Comfortable with recommendations if he can control them

### Pain Points

- Open slots are useless without partners
- Self-reported skill levels can be unreliable
- No-shows ruin the game
- Too many irrelevant notifications cause immediate opt-out

### Moments that Matter

- Finding an open game that is actually likely to happen
- Seeing host reliability and player level
- Being matched without exposing unnecessary personal information
- Building confidence after the first successful game

## Persona 4: Ms. Thao, the Venue Operator

### Descriptive

Thao is a 42-year-old manager of an eight-court badminton venue in Ho Chi Minh City. Evening occupancy is strong, but weekday daytime use is inconsistent. She manages bookings through calls, Zalo, and a spreadsheet and worries about double-booking and unnecessary discounts.

### Motivations

- Increase occupied court-hours and predictable cash flow
- Protect peak pricing
- Reduce manual coordination and no-shows
- Turn first-time players into repeat customers

### Interactions

- Uses a phone throughout the day and a desktop for accounting
- Needs a simple calendar and clear exceptions
- Trusts recommendations only when assumptions are visible
- Wants control over price floors, eligibility, and campaign limits

### Pain Points

- Inventory is not synchronized across channels
- Cancellations are difficult to refill
- She cannot tell whether a promotion created new demand
- Staff may resist changing established workflows

### Moments that Matter

- Importing or publishing inventory without disrupting operations
- Avoiding the first double-booking
- Seeing an empty slot refilled profitably
- Understanding whether a promotion increased margin, not just bookings
