# Design Thinking Document

*Purpose: This document is the foundational step. It shifts the focus away from "what features can we build?" to "what human problem are we solving?" It ensures the product is rooted in actual user needs rather than assumptions.*

## Empathize
*   **What it does:** Captures the emotional and practical reality of the user's current situation before your product exists.
*   **Why it's important:** If you don't understand the user's context, you will build a solution to the wrong problem. Empathy prevents "solution-first" thinking.
*   **Details to include:** Who are the users? What are their daily environments like? What are they feeling (anxious, bored, frustrated)? What workarounds are they currently using?
*   **Impact:** Defines the emotional target the product must hit.

## Define
*   **What it does:** Synthesizes the empathy findings into a single, actionable problem statement.
*   **Why it's important:** Provides a razor-sharp focus for the team. A poorly defined problem leads to bloated, unfocused products.
*   **Details to include:** The specific user segment, their core unmet need, and the friction preventing them from meeting that need. (e.g., "High school juniors lack personalized data to make confident career choices.")
*   **Impact:** Becomes the "North Star" against which all future feature ideas are judged.

## Ideate
*   **What it does:** Generates a wide volume of potential solutions to the defined problem.
*   **Why it's important:** Encourages "outside-the-box" thinking. Prevents the team from simply executing the very first idea that comes to mind.
*   **Details to include:** A brainstormed list of ideas (from wild to practical). Then, highlight the 1 or 2 ideas that best balance innovation with feasibility.
*   **Impact:** Expands the solution space, often leading to more creative product strategies.

## Prototype
*   **What it does:** Describes the low-fidelity or high-fidelity representation of the winning idea.
*   **Why it's important:** Ideas are cheap; prototypes make them tangible. It forces the team to think through actual user flows.
*   **Details to include:** What exactly will be built to test the idea? (e.g., A paper sketch, a clickable Figma mockup, a wizard-of-oz test). What is the core flow being demonstrated?
*   **Impact:** Saves immense engineering resources by validating concepts before coding begins.

## Test
*   **What it does:** Outlines the plan for putting the prototype in front of real users.
*   **Why it's important:** Validates assumptions. It's where the product team learns if they actually solved the problem defined in step 2.
*   **Details to include:** Who will you test with? What specific questions or feedback are you seeking? How will you measure success or failure?
*   **Impact:** Directs iteration. The feedback gathered here will fundamentally alter the PRD and Roadmap.
