# Product Artifacts: Best and Worst Practices

Creating effective product artifacts requires discipline. A poorly written artifact can misalign an entire engineering team and waste months of effort.

## 1. The PRD (Product Requirements Document)
✅ **Good Practice:** Write from the user's perspective focusing on *outcomes*. (e.g., "The system must allow the user to reset their password in under 3 clicks so they are not locked out.")
❌ **Bad Practice:** Dictating specific UI or technical solutions to the engineers. (e.g., "Put a blue button in the top right corner that calls the auth/reset API endpoint.")
⚠️ **Frequent Mistake:** Failing to define the "Out of Scope" section, leading to massive feature creep and missed deadlines.

## 2. The User Persona
✅ **Good Practice:** Base the persona on actual qualitative and quantitative data from user interviews and surveys. Keep the details relevant to your product domain.
❌ **Bad Practice:** Relying entirely on stereotypes or team assumptions. Including irrelevant details that don't impact the product (e.g., "Sarah's favorite color is blue" - unless you are designing a paint app, this doesn't matter).
⚠️ **Frequent Mistake:** Creating too many personas. If you have 10 personas, you are designing for nobody. Stick to 1 primary and 1-2 secondary personas.

## 3. The Product Roadmap
✅ **Good Practice:** Organizing the roadmap by "Themes" or "Capabilities" (e.g., "Frictionless Checkout Experience") and linking them to specific success metrics.
❌ **Bad Practice:** Creating a roadmap that is just a list of random features with hard deadlines (e.g., "Add Apple Pay on Oct 14th, Add PayPal on Nov 2nd").
⚠️ **Frequent Mistake:** Treating the roadmap as a locked-in contract rather than a living, strategic document that adapts to market changes.

## 4. The Prototype Plan
✅ **Good Practice:** Building the lowest-fidelity prototype necessary to answer your riskiest assumption. If a paper sketch can validate the flow, don't build a high-fidelity interactive mockup.
❌ **Bad Practice:** Trying to prototype every single edge case, settings menu, and error state. 
⚠️ **Frequent Mistake:** Creating a prototype that looks *too* finished. If a prototype looks like a final coded product, users focus their feedback on the color of buttons rather than the core functionality of the workflow.

## 5. The Pitch Deck
✅ **Good Practice:** Telling a narrative story. Starting with a deeply emotional or financially impactful pain point before revealing the product.
❌ **Bad Practice:** The "Frankenstein Deck." Pasting 50 dense bullet points and complex architecture diagrams onto a single slide.
⚠️ **Frequent Mistake:** Focusing 90% of the deck on *what* the product is (features), and ignoring *why* it matters (viability/desirability).
